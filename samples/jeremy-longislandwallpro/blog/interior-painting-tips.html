Prep Work Is 80% of the Job

This is the single most important thing I can tell you, and it's the thing most DIYers skip: preparation is everything. A professional painting contractor in Suffolk County will spend more time prepping a room than actually painting it. That's not inefficiency â€” that's the secret to a flawless finish.

Proper prep includes:

  • Cleaning the walls. Dust, grease, and grime prevent paint from bonding properly. Wipe down every surface with a damp cloth or TSP solution, especially in kitchens and bathrooms.
  • Filling holes and cracks. Every nail hole, ding, and hairline crack needs to be spackled, dried, and sanded smooth. Run your hand over the wall â€” if you can feel it, you'll see it through the paint.
  • Sanding glossy surfaces. If the existing paint has any sheen, lightly sand the entire surface with 150-grit sandpaper to give the new coat something to grip.
  • Taping and masking. Use quality painter's tape (not masking tape) along trim, ceilings, and any edge where two colors meet. Press the edge firmly to prevent bleed-through.
  • Protecting floors and furniture. Canvas drop cloths â€” not plastic. Plastic is slippery and paint pools on it. Canvas absorbs drips and stays put.

In the union, they drill this into you from day one: the paint is only as good as the surface underneath it. Skip the prep, and you'll see every flaw magnified once the paint dries.

Primer Isn't Optional

I can't tell you how many homes I've walked into across Port Jefferson, Centereach, and Commack where someone skipped primer and then wondered why their new paint looked uneven, patchy, or started peeling within a year.

Primer does three critical things:

  1. Seals the surface so paint adheres uniformly
  2. Blocks stains â€” water marks, smoke damage, ink, and tannin bleed from wood trim
  3. Provides a consistent base so your topcoat color is true and even

Always prime when you're covering a dark color with a lighter one, painting new drywall, covering repaired areas, or dealing with any kind of stain. A quality shellac-based primer like Zinsser B-I-N handles almost any situation. For new drywall, PVA primer is your best friend.

Cutting In vs. Rolling: The Right Order

Here's a technique debate that's been going on forever: do you cut in first or roll first? After three decades, I can tell you definitively â€” cut in first, then roll while the cut-in edge is still wet.

"Cutting in" means using an angled brush (I prefer a 2.5-inch Purdy) to paint the edges where a roller can't reach â€” along ceiling lines, corners, around trim, and next to door frames. The key is to keep a wet edge. If you cut in the entire room and then come back to roll, the cut-in areas will have dried and you'll see a visible band of different texture where the brush strokes meet the rolled area.

The professional approach: cut in one wall, then immediately roll that same wall before moving to the next. This keeps everything wet and blends together seamlessly.

For rolling technique, use a "W" pattern: roll the paint on in a large W shape, then fill in the area with even, overlapping strokes. Always maintain a wet edge and don't press too hard â€” let the roller do the work.

Choosing the Right Paint

Not all paint is created equal, and cheaper paint will cost you more in the long run. Here's what a professional painting contractor in Suffolk County actually uses:

  • Flat/matte â€” Best for ceilings and low-traffic areas. Hides imperfections well but marks easily and is harder to clean.
  • Eggshell â€” The most popular choice for living rooms, bedrooms, and dining rooms. Slight sheen, easy to clean, still forgiving on wall imperfections.
  • Satin â€” Ideal for kitchens, bathrooms, hallways, and kids' rooms. More durable and washable. Shows more wall imperfections than eggshell.
  • Semi-gloss â€” Best for trim, doors, cabinets, and molding. Very durable and easy to clean. Too shiny for large wall surfaces.

As for brands, I've used them all. Benjamin Moore Regal Select and Sherwin-Williams Duration are my go-to products. They cover better, level smoother, and last longer than budget paints. You'll get better coverage in fewer coats, which saves time and often makes the per-square-foot cost comparable to cheaper alternatives.

The Details That Matter

A few more tips from the field that most painting guides don't mention:

  • Box your paint. If you're using multiple gallons of the same color, pour them all into a five-gallon bucket and mix thoroughly. This eliminates any slight batch-to-batch color variation.
  • Don't paint in direct sunlight. Sun hitting a freshly painted wall causes it to dry too fast, leading to lap marks and uneven sheen.
  • Remove switch plates and outlet covers. It takes 30 seconds and makes a huge difference in the finished look. Paint around an outlet cover and it screams "amateur."
  • Two coats minimum. Always. Even if it looks good after one coat. The second coat provides the durability and uniform color depth that makes a paint job last.
  • Watch the humidity. Long Island humidity can wreak havoc on drying times. If it's above 70% humidity, expect longer dry times and plan accordingly. Don't apply a second coat until the first is fully cured.
